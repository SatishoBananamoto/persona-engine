METHODOLOGY
-----------
This report reviews every tunable constant in the persona-engine behavioral
layer against empirical findings from personality psychology and computational
linguistics. Where exact effect sizes exist, they are cited. Where the
literature provides directional evidence but no precise coefficient, the
recommendation is bounded by the range of plausible values from multiple
converging sources.

Key principle: In personality-to-language mapping, individual trait-behavior
correlations are reliably SMALL (r = .08-.15 for self-report, r = .18-.39
for observer-report). The "Kernel of Truth" meta-analysis (Tackman et al.,
2023, Psychological Bulletin) across 85,724 participants found that 52 LIWC
categories explain on average only 5.1% of personality variance from
self-report. This means modifier weights in the 0.1-0.4 range per trait
are empirically appropriate — anything above 0.5 per single trait is
likely too strong for a realistic simulation.

3. influences_verbosity() — Conscientiousness → detail orientation
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Current:
  adjusted = base_verbosity + (conscientiousness - 0.5) * 0.2
  Clamped [0.0, 1.0]
  Thresholds: <0.15=MINIMAL, <0.35=BRIEF, <0.65=MEDIUM, else=DETAILED

    fewer negative emotion words — NOT length.
  - PERSONAGE maps C primarily to planning language and structure quality,
    not to verbosity. [S1, S3]
  - The 0.2 modifier means C only shifts verbosity by +-0.1 from center,
    which is appropriately small.
  - Extraversion is actually the stronger predictor of word count/verbosity
    (r~.10-.15 for word count, [S7, S13]), but it is not included in this
    function.

Assessment: MODIFIER SIZE IS GOOD (0.2 scaling = +-0.1 max shift).
  But the function is MISSING extraversion influence on verbosity, which
  is empirically the stronger predictor.

Recommendation:
  Keep the C modifier at 0.2.
  ALSO add: + (extraversion - 0.5) * 0.15
  This reflects that E is a slightly weaker but significant predictor of
  verbose output (extraverts talk more, [S4, S5, S8, S13]).

  Verbosity thresholds: KEEP current values. The 4-bin structure is
  reasonable for discrete categorization.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
Current:
  return (extraversion - 0.5) * 0.4
  Range: -0.2 to +0.2

Empirical evidence:
  - Extraversion is positively correlated with self-disclosure, especially
    in social media contexts. Extraverts share more personal information
    and use more self-referencing language. [S5, S8]
  - PERSONAGE maps high extraversion to higher self-reference rates. [S1]
  - Pennebaker & King [S4] found extraverts use more social process words

  - The effect is real but SMALL. The meta-analytic correlation between

  - The +-0.2 range is appropriate for a modifier that gets ADDED to a

  - HOWEVER: Neuroticism also predicts self-disclosure (especially of
    negative content), with r~.10 for first-person singular pronouns
    [S7]. This is NOT currently modeled.

Assessment: GOOD — the +-0.2 range and 0.4 scaling factor are reasonable.
  Could consider adding a small neuroticism component.

Recommendation:
  KEEP (extraversion - 0.5) * 0.4  [= +-0.2 range]
  Consider adding: + neuroticism * 0.1  [= 0 to +0.1 range]
  Rationale: High neuroticism increases disclosure of negative personal
  content (rumination, venting). This is a secondary effect.

5. influences_directness() — Agreeableness inversely affects directness
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Current:
  modifier = (0.5 - agreeableness) * 0.3
  adjusted = base_directness + modifier
  Clamped [0.0, 1.0]

  At A=0 (very disagreeable): modifier = +0.15 (more direct)
  At A=1 (very agreeable): modifier = -0.15 (less direct)
  Range: +-0.15

Empirical evidence:
  - This is one of the BEST-SUPPORTED mappings in the system.
  - NEO-PI-R Agreeableness facet "Straightforwardness" directly measures

  - Schwartz et al. [S5] found that agreeable individuals use fewer swear

  - The DeYoung "Politeness" aspect of Agreeableness directly captures
    indirectness and tact.
  - The 0.3 scaling (+-0.15 range) is APPROPRIATE — this is a well-
    supported relationship but individual correlations are still modest
    (r~.15-.25 in behavioral studies).

Assessment: GOOD. The direction is correct, the magnitude is reasonable.

Recommendation:
  KEEP as is. The 0.3 scaling factor producing +-0.15 range is well-
  calibrated for the strength of the A-directness relationship.

Empirical evidence:

7. get_negative_tone_bias() — Neuroticism → negative tone
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Current:
  return neuroticism * 0.7
  Range: 0 to 0.7

Empirical evidence:
  - This is WELL-SUPPORTED. Neuroticism is THE strongest linguistic
    personality signal. The Kernel of Truth meta-analysis [S7] found
    neuroticism has the strongest correlations with negative emotion
    word categories (rho = .08-.14 for self-report, up to .39 for
    observer ratings).
  - Pennebaker & King [S4]: neurotics use more negative emotion words
    and fewer positive emotion words.
  - Schwartz et al. [S5]: High-N individuals use more negative words
    in social media posts.
  - PERSONAGE maps high neuroticism to negative content polarity. [S1]
  - PNAS study (2023): Neuroticism is associated with increased
    variability in negative emotion. People high in N show greater
    day-to-day fluctuation in negative affect.
  - The 0.7 multiplier means at N=1.0, there's a 70% bias toward
    negative tones. This is ARGUABLY TOO HIGH. Even at the extreme
    of neuroticism, people don't have negative tone 70% of the time.
    The empirical data shows N amplifies negative affect reactivity
    but doesn't make it the dominant mode.

Assessment: DIRECTION CORRECT, magnitude TOO HIGH.

Recommendation:
  return neuroticism * 0.5    # Was 0.7. Reduced.
  Rationale: Even at extreme neuroticism (N=1.0), the negative tone
  bias should be a strong tendency (0.5) but not overwhelming (0.7).
  At N=0.5 (average), this gives 0.25 — a mild negative skew, which
  matches the empirical finding of small-to-moderate effect sizes.

8. get_confidence_modifier() — C boost, N penalty on confidence
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Current:
  c_boost = (conscientiousness - 0.5) * 0.1
  n_penalty = neuroticism * 0.15
  adjusted = domain_proficiency + c_boost - n_penalty
  Clamped [0.1, 0.95]

  At C=1.0, N=0: +0.05 boost, 0 penalty
  At C=0.5, N=1.0: 0 boost, -0.15 penalty
  At C=0.5, N=0.5: 0 boost, -0.075 penalty

Empirical evidence:
  - Neuroticism-confidence relationship: Meta-analytic evidence shows
    N correlates with self-efficacy at r=-.35 [Furnham, 2024 longitudinal].
    N correlates with self-esteem at r=-.59 [multiple meta-analyses].
    This is one of the STRONGEST personality-outcome relationships.
  - Schaefer et al. [S9]: Extraversion predicted overconfidence; N did
    NOT significantly predict confidence independent of other traits.
    However, the self-efficacy literature shows N strongly predicts
    lower self-efficacy in the work domain (r=-.35).
  - C-confidence: Conscientiousness predicts self-efficacy at r~.25-.35
    [meta-analyses]. The relationship is positive and meaningful.
  - The current N penalty (0.15 max) is TOO SMALL given the empirical
    evidence. N's effect on confidence/self-efficacy is one of the
    strongest personality-outcome links in the literature.
  - The current C boost (0.05 max) is also slightly too small. C
    predicts self-efficacy at r~.30.

Assessment: DIRECTION CORRECT for both. N penalty TOO SMALL, C boost
  TOO SMALL.

Recommendation:
  c_boost = (conscientiousness - 0.5) * 0.15   # Was 0.1. Range: +-0.075
  n_penalty = neuroticism * 0.20                # Was 0.15. Range: 0-0.20
  Clamped [0.1, 0.95] — KEEP

  Rationale: The N→confidence relationship (r~.35 for self-efficacy)
  justifies a stronger penalty. The C→confidence relationship (r~.30)
  justifies a slightly larger boost. The combined effect at extreme
  values (C=1, N=0: +0.075; C=0, N=1: -0.275) is still within a
  plausible range for individual-difference modifiers.

  ALSO CONSIDER: Adding a small Extraversion boost (+ E * 0.05),
  since E predicts overconfidence [S9] and social confidence.

Current thresholds (sampling):
  - Stress > 0.6 AND N > 0.6 → ANXIOUS/CONCERNED
  - E > 0.7 → enthusiasm bonus of 0.2
  - O > 0.65 AND E > 0.6 → EAGER_ANTICIPATORY

  - E < 0.35 AND N > 0.45 → GUARDED_WARY
  - N > 0.7 → GRIEVING_SORROWFUL

Empirical evidence:
  - The two-dimensional valence-arousal model for affect is well-
    established (Russell, 1980; Barrett & Russell, 1999). Using
    valence + arousal + trait modifiers for tone selection is a
    sound design. [Standard in affective computing]
  - Neuroticism thresholds: The 0.6 threshold for stress-anxiety
    interaction is reasonable. N > 0.6 corresponds to roughly +0.5 SD
    above mean in a normalized population. [S10]
  - Extraversion thresholds: 0.7 for high-E bonus and 0.65 for trait
    interactions are appropriate "high" cutoffs. [S10, S12]
  - The multi-trait interactions (O+E for eagerness, E+low-N for
    playfulness, low-A for contempt) are creative and face-valid but
    lack specific empirical validation at these exact thresholds.
  - The PERSONAGE system validates that multi-trait interactions DO
    produce recognizably distinct personality styles, even though
    exact thresholds are somewhat arbitrary. [S1, S3]

Assessment: STRUCTURALLY SOUND. Thresholds are reasonable. Specific
  values are defensible but not precisely calibrated.

Recommendation:
  - KEEP most thresholds. They map well to standard "high"/"low"
    cutoffs in Big Five research.
  - Consider lowering the N > 0.7 grief threshold to N > 0.65.
    At 0.7, only ~15% of the population would ever reach this tone.
    At 0.65 it becomes ~20%, which is still appropriately rare.
  - The E > 0.7 enthusiasm bonus of 0.2 is reasonable but consider
    making it proportional: e_bonus = (E - 0.5) * 0.4 if E > 0.5.
    This gives a graded effect rather than a step function.

13. influences_mood_stability() — inverse Neuroticism
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Current:
  return 1.0 - neuroticism

Empirical evidence:
  - PNAS (2023): N is associated with increased variability in negative
    emotion across 13 longitudinal datasets (N=2,518). The relationship
    is robust.
  - However, the inversion is too symmetric. At N=0.5, mood stability
    = 0.5, which suggests "moderately unstable" for an average person.
    Normal-range individuals typically have fairly stable mood.

Assessment: TOO STRONG at midrange. Average N should yield above-
  average stability.

Recommendation:
  return 1.0 - neuroticism * 0.8   # Range: 0.2 to 1.0
  Rationale: At N=0.5 (average), stability = 0.6 (slightly above
  midpoint, reflecting that average people are somewhat stable). At
  N=1.0, stability = 0.2 (quite unstable). This better matches the
  empirical finding that mood instability is primarily a feature of
  HIGH neuroticism, not of average neuroticism.

17. baseline_valence (in apply_mood_drift)

  baseline_valence = 0.1 - (neuroticism * 0.2)
  Range: -0.1 (N=1) to 0.1 (N=0)

  Range: -0.05 (E=0, N=1) to 0.30 (E=1, N=0)
  Rationale: Adds the E contribution to positive affect baseline.
  The positivity offset (0.15) ensures most personas drift slightly
  positive, matching empirical population data.

Empirical evidence:
  - Cognitive biases have measurable but bounded effects on judgment.
    The confirmation bias literature shows effect sizes of d~0.3-0.5

  - Translating to a 0-1 scale modifier, 0.15 means a bias can shift
    a parameter by at most 15%. This is conservative.

Assessment: GOOD. The 0.15 cap is deliberately conservative and
  prevents biases from dominating persona behavior.

Recommendation: KEEP at 0.15. The bounded design is a feature.

24. cognitive_complexity * 0.6 + (1-need_for_closure) * 0.4
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Current (get_elasticity_from_cognitive_style):
  complexity_factor = cognitive_complexity * 0.6
  closure_factor = (1.0 - need_for_closure) * 0.4

  - Need for Closure (NFC) is inversely related to flexibility.

  - The 60/40 split between complexity and closure is not precisely
    calibrated by any study, but the relative ordering (complexity
    as primary) is correct.

Assessment: GOOD. Reasonable heuristic weighting.

25. confidence_adjustment — analytical penalty and closure boost
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Current:
  analytical_penalty = -0.05 if analytical_intuitive >= 0.5 else 0
  closure_boost = +0.08 if need_for_closure >= 0.7 else 0

Empirical evidence:
  - Analytical thinkers see more caveats, which can reduce expressed
    confidence. The -0.05 penalty is small and appropriate.
  - High NFC individuals seek certainty and express more confidence
    in their judgments. The +0.08 boost is reasonable.
  - Both are implemented as step functions (binary on/off), which
    is less realistic than gradual scaling.

Assessment: MAGNITUDES GOOD. Step functions are suboptimal.

Recommendation:
  analytical_penalty = -(analytical_intuitive - 0.5) * 0.1 if > 0.5
  closure_boost = (need_for_closure - 0.5) * 0.15 if > 0.5
  This makes both effects gradual rather than binary.
